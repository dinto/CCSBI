The Fruit Bat Project


After the spill of zoonotic diseases like Nipah and covid 19, human attitude towards bats and their roosts has shifted significantly. Many populations have been chased away from their native roost sites across Kerala, altering the population status and natural distribution of the species. For the conservation and proper management of bats, we have to understand their ecology, specifically roosting sites and feeding grounds. The fruit bat project is aimed at identifying and mapping the roosting sites of a common fruit bat, the Indian flying fox; Pteropus medius. You may find them roosting on tall, wide trees, mostly in disturbed habitats. Know More

Invasion watch India

Across the world, natural habitats are being lost to invasive species. In India, species like Lantana camara and Giant African snails are taking over natural habitats, causing irreversible damages. The distribution and rate of spread of such species should be monitored for their proper management. This project is aimed at mapping invasive flora and fauna across the country. This will help us understand the trends of invasive spread, with which we can develop local, species-specific management plans. You can contribute by recording any invasive species from anywhere within the country. Know More

Pollinator count


Loss of pollinator diversity has started to threaten the ecosystem stability as well as the world economy. Raging temperatures and loss of natural habitats make our diverse insect populations vulnerable. We might have already lost many populations, but we lack any scientific data as no proper monitoring for pollinators is carried out nationally. To avoid further decline we need to look at the effect of climate change on population and distribution trends of major pollinator species. This project is aimed at understanding the pollinator dependence on native plants, population and distribution status and the challenges faced by them. Follow our social media accounts for further updates.

Forest Biodiversity database

Our tropical climate makes our forests one of the most diverse ecosystems in the world. Effect of anthropogenic influences and climate change is concerning, shouting out the need for frequent monitoring of biodiversity throughout our protected areas. A biodiversity database for each protected area is being developed by carrying out bioblitz-like surveys across the state. Locality specific databases will be highly beneficial for identifying hotspots and maintaining vulnerable habitats. Data will be published in public data repositories facilitating easy access for further research and activities.

Institutional-Biodiversity database

Urban wildlife is the least studied biological resource around us. We currently lack data on locality specific urban biodiversity. A ward-level urban-biodiversity database is being generated in collaboration with local governing bodies. This data can be very useful in identifying biodiversity hotspots and creating efficient management policies.

Riverscaping

The Riverscaping project is aimed at understanding the ecological and environmental importance of riparian habitats. It is one of the most biologically diverse ecotones which acts as a transition zone between two major ecosystems. Biodiversity assessments will be carried out across the urban and wild riverbanks of Kerala with the help of citizen scientists. Results will give a clear idea about the significance of natural river banks in determining its surrounding communities
